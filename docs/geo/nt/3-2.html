3.2 Implementing Random Sampling

Summary Statement of Topic

How you can use random sampling in your daily life to get unbiased data.

Key Terms

  • Random Sample: A subset of a population for which each member of the population has an equal chance of being chosen
  • Population: The entire group of interest from which the sample is drawn
  • Sample: A specified part of a population, intended to represent the population as a whole
  • Simple Random Sample: The process of assigning a number to each member of the population under study, and then using a random number generator to pick the samples
  • Random: Everyone or everything in a population has an equal chance of being selected
  • Systematic Random Sampling: A type of probability sampling technique where there is an equal chance of selecting each unit from within the population when creating the sample
  • Cluster Sampling: Choosing representatives who are close to other representatives based on a particular factor such as location, age, color, size, etc.
  • Subgroups: Another name for Stratum
  • Stratum: Single category or sub-population out of a larger population
  • Weighting: A mathematical device used to give some elements more “weight” or influence on the result than others in the same set
  • Sample Size: The number of individuals or objects surveyed or tested in the study
  • Survey: A data collection method used to gather information about individuals' opinions, beliefs, or habits
  • Estimate: An approximate answer that makes sense given the problem
  • Average: Also known as the arithmetic mean

Example of Random Sampling in Action

Erina’s population is expected to be approximately 4,000, which is the estimated daily number of customers at her WalMart. We can estimate that for a population of 4,000, she will need a sample of 350 people.

To find the step size (n), we divide:
4000 ÷ 350 ≈ 11

Erina should roll a pair of dice, let that number of people go by, then question every 11th person out the door to see if they purchased sporting goods.
