6.7 Expected Value

Big Picture

**Expected value** (also known as the mean or expectation) is a concept used in probability theory to describe the long-term average or mean value of a random variable. It is calculated by multiplying each possible outcome of a random variable by the probability of that outcome, then summing these values. The expected value represents the central tendency or "average" of a random process.

Key Terms

  • Value: A possible outcome or result of a probability experiment.
  • Probability: The chance or likelihood that a particular outcome will occur, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
  • Outcome: A possible end result of a probability experiment.
  • Experiment: The process of collecting data or making observations in a random process.
  • Probability Distribution: A table, graph, or chart showing all possible values of a random variable and their associated probabilities.

Summary

The **expected value** of a random variable \(X\) is calculated as: E(X) = Σ [x * P(x)] where: - \(x\) is each possible outcome of the random variable, - \(P(x)\) is the probability of that outcome occurring, - The sum (Σ) is taken over all possible values of \(x\).

The expected value provides an average or mean value that you would expect to observe over many repetitions of an experiment. In many cases, it represents the **center of the distribution** of the random variable.

Example: Rolling a Fair Die

Consider an experiment where we roll a **fair 6-sided die**. The possible outcomes are the numbers \(1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6\). The probability of each outcome is equal, with \(P(x) = \frac{1}{6}\) for all \(x\).

To find the **expected value** of the roll, we use the formula: E(X) = Σ [x * P(x)] Substituting the values: 

E(X) = (1 * 1/6) + (2 * 1/6) + (3 * 1/6) + (4 * 1/6) + (5 * 1/6) + (6 * 1/6)
E(X) = (1 + 2 + 3 + 4 + 5 + 6) / 6
E(X) = 21 / 6
E(X) = 3.5

So, the expected value of a roll of the die is **3.5**. This means that, on average, you would expect to roll a 3.5 over many trials, though it's not possible to roll a 3.5 on a single die roll. The expected value simply gives the average outcome over a large number of trials.

Conclusion

The **expected value** is a key concept in probability and statistics. It helps to summarize the central tendency of a random variable, and is often used to make decisions in uncertain situations. By understanding how to compute the expected value, you can better understand the long-term average outcomes of random processes and experiments.
