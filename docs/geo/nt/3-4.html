3.4 Cluster Sampling

Summary Statement of Topic

Helps to achieve a representative sample when the population is too big for you to choose a sample individually, so you choose in clusters.

Key Terms

  • Population: The entire group of interest from which the sample is drawn
  • Simple Random Sample: The process of assigning a number to each member of the population, and then using a random number generator to pick the samples
  • Representative Sample: A smaller number of members of a population whose responses to events model those of the entire population
  • Random: Everyone or everything in a population has an equal chance of being selected, the selection can be said to occur at random
  • Clusters: A naturally occurring subgroup of a population
  • Data: Information that has been collected to represent real life situations, usually in number form
  • Sample: A specified part of a population, intended to represent the population as a whole
  • Poll: Question members in a group regarding a specific topic
  • Survey: A data collection method used to gather information from individuals
  • Bias: A desire to achieve a specific result from a particular study, regardless of the data
  • Estimate: An approximate answer that is reasonable or makes sense given the problem
  • Arithmetic Mean: Also called the Average; Arithmetic Mean = x1 + x2 + ... + n

Example of Cluster Sampling

Population: Carnegie High School
Information Required: Favorite Color
Steps:
1. Have the grades as different groups (clusters).
2. Randomly choose 2 classes from each grade.
3. Randomly choose 2 class periods from each class.
4. Ask the students in each class their favorite color.

This way, instead of surveying the entire school, you focus on representative clusters to gather the information.

Additional Notes

Cluster sampling can save time and resources when dealing with a large population. The key is to ensure that the clusters are representative of the entire population.
