5.7 Probability of Intersection

Big Picture

The probability of intersection helps determine the likelihood of two events happening simultaneously or together. The intersection represents the common outcomes between two events, often visualized with a Venn diagram. This is a key concept in understanding how two events might be related and how they influence each other in probability experiments.

Key Terms

  • Venn Diagram: A diagram showing the relationships between different sets, especially their intersections and unions.
  • Outcomes: The possible results of an experiment.
  • Sample Space: The set of all possible outcomes of an experiment.
  • Conditional Probability: The probability of an event occurring given that another event has already occurred.
  • Event: A set of one or more outcomes of a probability experiment.
  • Independent Events: The occurrence of one event does not affect the probability of the other event.
  • Mutually Inclusive: Events that can happen at the same time.
  • Probability: The likelihood that an event will occur, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percent.
  • Intersection ( ∩ ): The common outcomes between two events; represented by the word "and".
  • Union ( ∪ ): The combination of two sets, representing the outcomes of "either or both" events.

Important Formula

The probability of the intersection of two events A and B, denoted by \( P(A \cap B) \), is calculated using the formula:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B | A)

Where:

  • P(A) is the probability of event A occurring
  • P(B | A) is the probability of event B occurring, given that event A has already occurred

Example: Drawing Cards from a Deck

Suppose we are drawing two cards from a deck of 52 cards, without replacement. Let event A be drawing a heart, and event B be drawing a queen. We want to find the probability of drawing both a heart and a queen.

We know that: - P(A) = 13/52 (since there are 13 hearts in a deck) - P(B | A) = 1/51 (since there is one queen of hearts, and there are 51 cards left after drawing one heart)

Using the formula for the intersection of two events:

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) * P(B | A)

P(A ∩ B) = (13/52) * (1/51) = 13/2652 ≈ 0.0049

So, the probability of drawing both a heart and a queen is approximately 0.49%.

Conclusion

The probability of intersection is a critical concept when dealing with dependent events. It allows us to compute the likelihood of two events occurring together, which is essential for understanding more complex scenarios in probability. Whether drawing cards, rolling dice, or conducting experiments, the intersection of events plays a significant role in analyzing outcomes.
