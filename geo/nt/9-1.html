9.1 Transformations

Summary Statement of Topic

Transformations in math refer to operations that change the position, size, or shape of a figure. These include translations, rotations, reflections, and dilations, each of which manipulates the figure in a specific way. The figure's appearance may change, but it retains its fundamental properties.

Key Terms

  • Rotations: A transformation that turns a figure around a fixed point, maintaining its shape and size.
  • Reflections: Transformations that create a mirror image of a figure. The resulting image is a "flipped" version of the original.
  • Plane: A flat, two-dimensional surface, often represented as an infinite sheet of paper.
  • Point: A specific location in space, usually identified by coordinates on a grid. It has no size or shape.
  • Triangle: A polygon with three sides and three angles, a fundamental shape in geometry.
  • Angles: Formed by two rays that meet at a common endpoint called the vertex. Angles are measured in degrees.
  • Line Segment: A finite part of a line, having two distinct endpoints. A line segment is straight and has a definite length.

Important Notes

Transformations help in understanding how shapes move and change. For example, rotating a triangle does not alter its shape, but its position changes. Similarly, reflecting a shape across a line results in a mirror image, preserving the size but flipping the orientation. These concepts are essential in geometry, particularly in symmetry and pattern recognition.
