8.10 Volume of Solids

Summary Statement of Topic

The volume of a solid represents the amount of space it occupies. Each type of solid has a unique formula to calculate its volume, depending on its shape. For instance, cubes, rectangular prisms, cylinders, spheres, cones, and pyramids each have specific volume formulas.

Key Terms

  • Prism: A 3D figure with 2 congruent bases in parallel planes, and other faces are rectangles.
  • Pyramid: A 3D shape with a polygonal base and triangular faces meeting at a vertex.
  • Cylinder: A 3D solid with congruent circular bases in parallel planes, often with a radius and height.
  • Cone: A 3D figure with a circular base and a single vertex.
  • Sphere: A round 3D solid where all points on the surface are equidistant from the center.
  • Composite Solid: A solid formed by combining two or more different solids.
  • Dimensions: Measurements defining the size and shape of a solid figure.
  • Measure: The distance or length between two points in space.
  • Space: The set of all points extending in three dimensions.
  • Cubic Units: Units used to measure the volume of three-dimensional solids.
  • Congruent: Figures that are identical in size, shape, and measure.
  • Parallel: Lines that never intersect and always maintain the same slope.
  • Area: The space inside the perimeter of a two-dimensional figure.
  • Height: The perpendicular distance from the base to the top of a solid or object.
  • Three-Dimensional: A figure that has length, width, and height or depth.
  • Polygon: A simple closed figure with at least three straight sides.
  • Vertex: The point of intersection of two lines or the corner of a polygon or solid.
  • Edge: A line segment that is the intersection of two faces in a solid figure.
  • Point: A specific location in space, often represented as a coordinate.
  • Radius: The distance from the center to any point on the surface of a circle or sphere.
  • Ratio: A comparison of two quantities.
  • Set: A collection of objects, such as numbers, points, or geometric shapes.
  • Distance: The length between two points in space.
  • Center: The point in the middle of a circle or sphere, equidistant from all points on the surface.
  • Rectangular Prism: A prism with rectangular bases and rectangular faces.
  • Face: A flat surface on a solid figure.

Example of Volume Calculation

Let's calculate the volume of a cylinder. The formula for the volume of a cylinder is:

Volume of a Cylinder: V = πr²h

Suppose the radius (r) of the base is 3 units and the height (h) is 5 units.

Volume = π × (3)² × 5 = π × 9 × 5 = 45π ≈ 141.37 cubic units.

Example of Composite Solid Volume

Consider a composite solid formed by a cone and a cylinder. To calculate the total volume, we calculate the volume of each shape separately and then add them together.

For the cylinder, use the formula V = πr²h. Suppose the radius (r) is 4 units, and the height (h) is 10 units.

Volume of Cylinder = π × (4)² × 10 = π × 16 × 10 = 160π ≈ 502.65 cubic units.

For the cone, use the formula V = (1/3)πr²h. The radius is 4 units, and the height is 6 units.

Volume of Cone = (1/3)π × (4)² × 6 = (1/3)π × 16 × 6 = 32π ≈ 100.53 cubic units.

Total Volume = Volume of Cylinder + Volume of Cone = 502.65 + 100.53 = 603.18 cubic units.

Important Notes

The volume of solids plays a critical role in various applications such as architecture, engineering, and design. Understanding how to calculate the volume of different solids is essential for determining the space they occupy, which can impact everything from construction to packaging.
