9.10 Composite Transformations and Reflection over y = ax and y = ax + b

Big Picture

Composite transformations involve applying multiple transformations in sequence, such as reflections, rotations, and translations. Reflection over lines like y = ax or y = ax + b flips a figure over a line with a given slope (a) and intercept (b). This process preserves the shape and size of the figure.

Key Terms

  • Rigid Transformation: A transformation that does not change the size or shape of a figure, also known as an isometry or congruence transformation.
  • Congruent: Figures that are identical in size, shape, and measure, even if rotated or reflected.
  • Plane: A flat, two-dimensional surface with infinite area.
  • Reflection: A transformation that flips a figure over a line, creating a mirror image.
  • Rotation: A transformation that turns a figure around a point without changing its shape or size.
  • Line: A straight one-dimensional figure made up of infinitely many points extending in opposite directions.
  • Parallel: Two lines that never intersect and always have the same slope.
  • Perpendicular: Lines that intersect at a 90-degree angle, with slopes that are opposite reciprocals of each other.

Example of Reflection over y = ax

Consider a triangle with vertices A(1, 2), B(3, 4), and C(5, 6). If we reflect this triangle over the line y = x, the reflected coordinates of A, B, and C would be: 

A'(2, 1), B'(4, 3), C'(6, 5)

This transformation preserves the shape and size of the triangle, but flips it across the line y = x.

Example of Composite Transformation

Let's perform a composite transformation: first, a reflection over the line y = x, followed by a rotation of 90 degrees around the origin. For a point P(x, y): 

Step 1: Reflect over y = x → P'(y, x)
Step 2: Rotate 90 degrees around the origin → P''(-x, y)

After applying both transformations, we get the final coordinates of the point P''.

Key Takeaways

Understanding composite transformations and reflections allows us to manipulate figures in space without altering their size or shape. By applying multiple transformations in sequence, we can obtain new positions and orientations for figures. Reflections over lines such as y = ax or y = ax + b provide powerful tools to flip figures across different lines, preserving their congruency.
