7.4 Z-Scores II

Big Picture

**Z-scores** tell us how many standard deviations a particular value is from the mean of a distribution. This is a crucial concept in statistics for comparing values across different normal distributions.

**Z-scores** are not just about telling how far a data point is from the mean but also how this distance translates into probabilities, based on the properties of a normal distribution.

Key Terms

  • Z-Score: The number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean. Z = (X - μ) / σ
  • Normal Curve: The bell-shaped curve that represents a normal distribution of data.
  • Empirical Rule: States that in a normal distribution, approximately 68% of values lie within 1 standard deviation (σ), 95% within 2 standard deviations, and 99.7% within 3 standard deviations.
  • Standard Deviation: A measure of how much data deviates from the mean.
  • Sample: A subset of data taken from a larger population intended to represent that population.

Important Info

The **Empirical Rule** helps you understand the proportion of data within a certain number of standard deviations from the mean in a normal distribution.

The rule states:

  • 68% of the data lies within **1 standard deviation** of the mean (±1σ)
  • 95% lies within **2 standard deviations** of the mean (±2σ)
  • 99.7% lies within **3 standard deviations** of the mean (±3σ)
The **Z-score formula** is used to calculate how many standard deviations a value (X) is from the mean (μ): 
Z = (X - μ) / σ

Example

Suppose you have a **normal distribution** where the mean (μ) of test scores is **75** and the standard deviation (σ) is **10**. If a student scores **85**, you can calculate the Z-score to see how far above the average they scored.

Using the Z-score formula: 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Z = (85 - 75) / 10
Z = 10 / 10
Z = 1
The Z-score of 1 means that the student's score is **1 standard deviation above** the mean.

Application of Z-Scores

Z-scores are often used in hypothesis testing and probability calculations. For instance, you can use the Z-score to determine the probability that a particular value occurs in a normal distribution.

For example, a Z-score of **2** means that a value lies 2 standard deviations above the mean. Using **Z-tables**, you can determine that this corresponds to roughly **97.72%** of the values being below that point.

Conclusion

Z-scores are an essential tool for comparing data points within normal distributions. They help determine how likely or unusual a given data point is, based on its position relative to the mean. A Z-score of 0 indicates that the data point is exactly at the mean, while a positive or negative Z-score tells you how far it deviates.
