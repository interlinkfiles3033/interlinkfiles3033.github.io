5.6 Probability of Unions

Big Picture

The probability of unions helps us understand the likelihood of either one or more events happening in a probability experiment. The union of two sets is the set of outcomes that belong to either one or both of the events. This concept is useful in many everyday situations where we are interested in the occurrence of one or more events.

Key Terms

  • Event: A set of one or more possible outcomes in a probability experiment.
  • Outcomes: The possible end results of an experiment.
  • Union (⋃): The combination of two or more sets into one larger set, which contains all the elements from both sets.
  • Probability: The chance that an event will occur, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
  • Intersection: The common outcomes between two events; often represented by the word "and."
  • Venn Diagram: A diagram that visually represents the relationships between different sets, including unions and intersections.

Important Formula

The probability of the union of two events A and B, denoted as P(A ∪ B), is given by the formula:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

Where:

  • P(A) is the probability of event A occurring
  • P(B) is the probability of event B occurring
  • P(A ∩ B) is the probability of both A and B occurring (the intersection of A and B)

Example: Drawing Cards from a Deck

Let's say we are drawing one card from a deck of 52 cards. We want to know the probability of drawing either a heart or a spade. Let event A be drawing a heart, and event B be drawing a spade. The probability of event A (P(A)) is 13/52 (since there are 13 hearts in a deck), and the probability of event B (P(B)) is also 13/52. Since there is no overlap between hearts and spades, the intersection of A and B (P(A ∩ B)) is 0.

Using the formula for the union of two events:

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B)

P(A ∪ B) = 13/52 + 13/52 - 0 = 26/52 = 1/2

So, the probability of drawing either a heart or a spade is 1/2, or 50%.

Conclusion

Understanding the probability of unions is an essential concept in probability theory. It allows us to calculate the likelihood of one or more events occurring together, which can be applied in various everyday situations, such as drawing cards, rolling dice, or predicting outcomes in experiments.
