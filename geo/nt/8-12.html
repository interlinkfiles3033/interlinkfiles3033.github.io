8.12 The Cross Sections of Solids

Summary Statement of Topic

Cross sections of solids are the two-dimensional shapes formed when a solid object is sliced by a plane. These cross sections provide a way to explore the internal structure of solids, helping to visualize how their shapes change when sliced at different angles or heights.

Key Terms

  • Cross-Section: The intersection of a three-dimensional solid with a plane, resulting in a 2D shape.
  • Intersection: The common elements between two sets in mathematics, or the point where a solid and a plane meet.
  • Space: The set of all points extending in three dimensions.
  • Plane: A flat, two-dimensional surface, conceptually infinite in area.
  • Face: A flat surface on a solid figure.
  • Pyramid: A 3D shape with a polygonal base and triangular faces that meet at a single vertex.
  • Vertex: The point where two lines or edges meet in geometry.
  • Parallel: Lines that lie in the same plane and never intersect, with the same slope.
  • Perpendicular: Lines that intersect at a 90-degree angle, where their slopes are opposite reciprocals.
  • Hexagon: A six-sided polygon.
  • Dimensions: Measurements that define the shape and size of a figure.
  • Rectangular Prism: A prism with two rectangular bases and four rectangular faces.
  • Rectangle: A parallelogram with four right angles.
  • Prism: A 3D figure with two congruent bases in parallel planes and rectangular faces connecting them.
  • Area: The space within the perimeter of a two-dimensional figure.
  • Square: A rectangle with four congruent sides and four right angles.
  • Height: The perpendicular distance from the base to the opposite vertex in a triangle.
  • Pentagon: A five-sided polygon.
  • Congruent: Figures that are identical in size, shape, and measure.
  • Cylinder: A 3D shape with congruent circular bases and a set height.
  • Radius: The distance from the center of a circle to its outer rim.
  • Circle: A set of all points equidistant from a central point.
  • Sphere: A round 3D solid where all points on the surface are equidistant from the center.
  • Triangle: A polygon with three sides and three angles.
  • Center: The central point of a circle, equidistant from all points on the circle's surface.
  • Equilateral Triangle: A triangle with three equal sides and angles.
  • Edge: A line segment formed by the intersection of two faces of a solid.
  • Vertices: Points where line segments or edges meet in a solid.
  • Polygon: A closed figure with at least three straight sides.
  • Cone: A 3D figure with a circular base and one vertex at the top.

Example of Cross Section of a Cylinder

Consider a vertical cylinder. When you slice the cylinder horizontally, the cross section is a circle. When you slice it vertically, the cross section is a rectangle. The orientation of the slice determines the resulting 2D shape.

Example of Cross Section of a Pyramid

When you slice a square pyramid horizontally, the cross section is a smaller square. When you slice it diagonally, the cross section is a triangle, and if you slice vertically from one side to the apex, you get a triangle.

Important Notes

Cross sections offer valuable insight into the internal structure and geometry of 3D solids. Understanding how the shape changes when sliced in different ways helps in fields like engineering, architecture, and design.
