9.9 Rotation Symmetry

Big Picture

Rotation Symmetry is a type of symmetry where an object can fit onto itself multiple times while being rotated through 360 degrees. It is sometimes called radial symmetry.

If a figure can be rotated about a central point and still match its original shape after a certain number of degrees, it has rotational symmetry. For example, a square has rotational symmetry of 90°, 180°, 270°, and 360°.

Key Terms

  • Rotational Symmetry: A figure has rotational symmetry when it can be rotated less than 360° and still look the same.
  • Angle: A geometric figure formed by two rays that connect at a single point or vertex.
  • Rotation: A transformation that turns a figure around a fixed point, creating an image.
  • Center: The fixed point around which a figure rotates.
  • Rectangle: A parallelogram with four right angles.
  • Square: A parallelogram with four right angles and four congruent sides.
  • Trapezoid: A quadrilateral with exactly one pair of parallel opposite sides.
  • Circle: The set of all points that are equidistant from a center point.

Square

A square has rotational symmetry of 90°, 180°, 270°, and 360°. Each of these rotations brings the square back to its original position.

Rectangle

A rectangle has rotational symmetry of 180°, meaning that after a 180° rotation, it still appears the same.

Pentagon

A pentagon has rotational symmetry of 72°, 144°, 216°, and 288°, allowing it to fit onto itself multiple times during a full rotation.
