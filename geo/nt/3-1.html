3.1 Identifying Random Sampling

Summary Statement of Topic

Shows how Random Sampling works without bias and how you can identify it in real life.

Key Terms

  • Random Sampling: Involves choosing representatives by rolling a die, for instance
  • Population: The entire group of interest from which the sample is drawn
  • Sample: A specified part of a population, intended to represent the population as a whole
  • Bias: Refers to a desire to achieve a specific result from a particular study, regardless of the data
  • Random: Everyone or everything in a population has an equal chance of being selected
  • Random Sample: A subset of a population for which each member of the population has an equal chance of being chosen
  • Simple Random Sample: The process of assigning a number to each member of the population under study, and then using a random number generator to pick the samples
  • Survey: A data collection method used to gather information about individuals' opinions, beliefs, or habits
  • Convenience Sampling: Refers to the process of choosing a sample based on members who are easily accessible

Example of Random Sampling

Each student is assigned a number, and a die is rolled. Starting with the number rolled, every 4th student is chosen until the quota is met.

Assume the number rolled is 2. In this case, this is a valid random sampling method, specifically a **systematic random sample**, because each student has an equal opportunity to be chosen as any other.
