8.5 Quadrilaterals - Definition, Types, Properties, Interactives, and Examples

Big Picture

A **quadrilateral** is a two-dimensional polygon that consists of **four sides** and **four angles**. Quadrilaterals can be classified into several types based on their properties. Examples include **rectangles**, **squares**, **parallelograms**, and **trapezoids**. Quadrilaterals are fundamental in geometry and are often used in architecture, art, and engineering.

Key Terms

  • Polygon: A simple closed figure with at least three straight sides.
  • Quadrilateral: A closed figure with four sides and four vertices.
  • Angles: A geometric figure formed by two rays that connect at a single point or vertex.
  • Vertices: Points where line segments intersect.
  • Diagonals: A line segment in a polygon that connects nonconsecutive or nonadjacent vertices.
  • Convex Polygon: Contains no interior angles greater than 180 degrees.
  • Congruent: Figures that are identical in size, shape, and measure.
  • Kite: A quadrilateral with two pairs of adjacent congruent sides.
  • Angle Bisector: A ray that splits an angle into two congruent smaller angles.
  • Parallel Lines: Lines that lie in the same plane and never intersect. These lines always have the same slope.
  • Trapezoid: A quadrilateral with exactly one pair of parallel opposite sides.
  • Isosceles Trapezoid: A trapezoid where the non-parallel sides are congruent in length.
  • Parallelogram: A quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel sides.
  • Rectangle: A parallelogram with four right angles.
  • Rhombus: A parallelogram with all sides congruent.
  • Square: A parallelogram with four right angles and four congruent sides.
  • Adjacent Angles: Two angles are adjacent if they share a side and vertex.

Important Info

- The sum of the interior angles of any quadrilateral is **360°**.
- In a **parallelogram**, opposite sides are parallel and equal in length. Opposite angles are also congruent.
- In a **rectangle**, all angles are 90° and opposite sides are congruent.
- In a **square**, all angles are 90°, and all sides are of equal length.
- A **trapezoid** has one pair of opposite sides that are parallel.
- A **kite** has two pairs of adjacent congruent sides.

Example

Let's work with a **parallelogram** where the opposite sides are **4 cm** and **6 cm**. If the height of the parallelogram is **3 cm**, the area can be calculated as: 

Area = base × height
Area = 6 cm × 3 cm
Area = 18 cm²
Therefore, the area of the parallelogram is **18 cm²**.

  • Draw a **square** with a known side length and calculate its area: Area = side².

**Tip**: Use a **protractor** to measure the angles and verify properties like perpendicularity or parallelism in your drawings.

Conclusion

Quadrilaterals are an essential part of geometry, classified based on their side lengths and angle properties. Understanding the characteristics of different types of quadrilaterals helps in solving real-world problems and constructing geometric designs. Whether it's calculating area, identifying types based on properties, or using quadrilaterals in constructions, their applications are vast and fundamental.
