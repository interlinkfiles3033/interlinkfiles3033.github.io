7.5 Z-Scores III

Big Picture

**Z-scores** are used to describe the relative position of a data point within a normal distribution by measuring how many standard deviations the point is from the mean. Z-scores are important for comparing different data sets and analyzing the likelihood of occurrences in those sets.

The use of Z-scores is prevalent in statistics, especially in hypothesis testing, probability calculations, and standardization of scores from different distributions.

Key Terms

  • Z-score: The number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean. Formula: Z = (X - μ) / σ
  • Distribution: A description of the values that a random variable can take and their corresponding probabilities.
  • Standard Deviation: A measure of the spread or dispersion of a data set.
  • Random: When every individual or element has an equal chance of being selected from the population.
  • Estimate: An approximation of a value, which is often used when exact calculations are difficult.

Important Info

The **Z-score formula** allows us to calculate how many standard deviations a particular data point (X) is away from the mean (μ) of a dataset, using the standard deviation (σ): 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Where:
  • **X** is the value you are calculating the Z-score for
  • **μ** is the mean of the data set
  • **σ** is the standard deviation of the data set

Example

Suppose you are analyzing the heights of a group of people, and you know that the average height (mean, μ) is **170 cm**, with a standard deviation (σ) of **10 cm**. You want to calculate the Z-score for someone who is **180 cm** tall.

Using the **Z-score formula**: 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Z = (180 - 170) / 10
Z = 10 / 10
Z = 1
The Z-score is **1**, which means that the height of 180 cm is **1 standard deviation above** the mean height.

Application of Z-Scores

Z-scores can be used to compare data points from different distributions. By transforming values into Z-scores, you standardize them, making it possible to compare data from different scales.

For instance, a Z-score of **2** corresponds to a value that is **2 standard deviations above** the mean. You can use **Z-tables** to find the probability associated with a particular Z-score. For example, a Z-score of 2 corresponds to approximately **97.72%** of the data being less than this value in a standard normal distribution.

Conclusion

Z-scores are an essential statistical tool that help to quantify how far away a data point is from the mean in terms of standard deviations. By calculating the Z-score, you can understand the relative standing of a value within a distribution and make informed decisions based on this information.
