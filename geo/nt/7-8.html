7.8 Approximating the Binomial Distribution

Big Picture

**Approximating the Binomial Distribution** involves using the **normal distribution** to estimate probabilities for a binomial distribution under certain conditions. This method is especially useful when dealing with large sample sizes, as computing the exact binomial probability can become complex.

When the sample size is large and the probability of success is neither too close to 0 nor 1, the binomial distribution can be approximated by a normal distribution using the **mean** and **standard deviation** of the binomial distribution.

Key Terms

  • Binomial Distribution: A distribution produced by an experiment with two possible outcomes, where there is a fixed number of trials, and each trial is independent.
  • Probability: The chance of a certain outcome occurring, expressed as a fraction, decimal, or percentage.
  • Standard Deviation: A measure of the spread of a data set. The larger the standard deviation, the more spread out the data is.
  • Combination: A selection of a specified number of objects from a set, where the order of selection does not matter.
  • Experiment: The process of taking measurements or making observations under controlled conditions.

Important Info

To approximate a binomial distribution using the normal distribution, the following conditions should be met:

  • The sample size (n) should be large enough.
  • The probability of success (p) and failure (1 - p) should not be too small (e.g., both np and n(1 - p) should be greater than 5).

The **mean** and **standard deviation** of the binomial distribution can be calculated as: 
Mean (μ) = n * p
Standard Deviation (σ) = √(n * p * (1 - p))
Where: - **n** is the number of trials. - **p** is the probability of success on a single trial.

Example

Suppose a company has a 60% chance of success (p = 0.6) on each trial, and you are conducting 100 trials (n = 100). The binomial distribution can be approximated by a normal distribution with: 

Mean (μ) = n * p = 100 * 0.6 = 60
Standard Deviation (σ) = √(n * p * (1 - p)) = √(100 * 0.6 * 0.4) ≈ 4.9
Now, to calculate the probability of having exactly 65 successes, we would use the normal distribution to approximate the binomial probability, using the mean and standard deviation calculated above.

Application of Binomial Approximation

The **binomial approximation** using the normal distribution is especially useful when n is large. For example, if you need to find the probability of getting between 60 and 70 successes in the 100 trials, you would: 1. Convert the binomial probability into a normal probability using the **continuity correction** (add 0.5 to the value to make the normal curve fit the discrete nature of the binomial distribution). 2. Use the **Z-score formula** to standardize the values: 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Where: - **X** is the number of successes. - **μ** is the mean. - **σ** is the standard deviation.

Conclusion

Approximating the binomial distribution with a normal distribution is a powerful technique for estimating probabilities, especially when dealing with large sample sizes. By calculating the mean and standard deviation of the binomial distribution and using the normal distribution, you can simplify complex binomial probability problems, provided the necessary conditions are met.
