7.3 Z-Scores

Big Picture

A **Z-score** tells you how many standard deviations a data point is away from the mean of the data set. Z-scores are essential for understanding how far away a value is from the average, and they can be used to compare scores across different data sets.

The Z-score formula is helpful to standardize values, making it easier to compare different distributions.

Key Terms

  • Z-Score: The number of standard deviations a data point is from the mean. Z = (X - μ) / σ
  • Standard Deviation: A measure of spread in a data set, showing how much the values deviate from the mean.
  • Empirical Rule: States that for a normal distribution, 68% of the data lies within 1σ, 95% within 2σ, and 99.7% within 3σ.

Important Info

The **Empirical Rule** applies to normal distributions and tells you what percentage of data lies within standard deviations from the mean:

- **68%** of data lies within **1 standard deviation** (±1σ) - **95%** of data lies within **2 standard deviations** (±2σ) - **99.7%** of data lies within **3 standard deviations** (±3σ)

The **Z-score** formula is as follows: 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Where:
Z = Z-score
X = Data point
μ = Mean
σ = Standard deviation

Example

Let's say we have a dataset where the **mean** score on a test is **70**, and the **standard deviation** is **10**. If a student scores **85**, we can calculate their Z-score to understand how far above the average they scored.

Using the Z-score formula: 

Z = (X - μ) / σ
Z = (85 - 70) / 10
Z = 15 / 10
Z = 1.5
So, the student’s score is **1.5 standard deviations above** the mean.

Conclusion

**Z-scores** are valuable for comparing different data points or distributions, as they standardize the data. They allow you to assess how unusual or typical a given value is in comparison to the mean of the data set. A Z-score of 0 means the data point is exactly at the mean, while positive or negative Z-scores indicate how far above or below the mean a point lies.
