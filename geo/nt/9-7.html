9.7 Rotation

Big Picture

A rotation is a transformation by which a figure is turned around a fixed point to create an image.

Key Terms

  • Rotation: A transformation that turns a figure on the coordinate plane a certain number of degrees about a given point without changing the shape or size of the figure.
  • Angles: A geometric figure formed by two rays that connect at a single point or vertex.
  • Parallel Lines: Two or more lines that lie in the same plane and never intersect. Parallel lines will always have the same slope.
  • Plane: A flat, two-dimensional surface. It can be conceptualized as a sheet of paper of infinite area.
  • Point: A location in space that does not have size or shape. A point is often identified as a coordinate on a grid.
  • Rigid Transformation: A transformation that does not change the size or shape of a figure, also known as an isometry or congruence transformation.
  • Circle: The set of all points that are the same distance away from a specific point, called the center.
  • Center: The point that defines the location of a circle. All points on the circle are equidistant from the center of the circle.
  • Radius: The distance from the center to the outer rim of a circle.
  • Distance: The length of the route between two points.
  • Corresponding: The corresponding sides between two triangles are sides in the same relative position.
  • Parallelogram: A quadrilateral with two pairs of parallel sides. A parallelogram may be a rectangle, a rhombus, or a square.
  • Measure: Determines how far apart two geometric objects are by using a number line or ruler.
  • Center of Rotation: The fixed point around which the rest of the plane rotates.
  • Origin: The point of intersection of the x and y axes on the Cartesian plane, with coordinates (0,0).

Example of Rotation

Let's rotate a triangle around the origin by 90 degrees counterclockwise. The triangle with vertices at (1, 1), (3, 1), and (2, 3) will have new coordinates after the rotation.

For a 90-degree counterclockwise rotation, the new coordinates of a point (x, y) will be (-y, x).

After rotating the vertices, the new coordinates will be (-1, 1), (-1, 3), and (-3, 2).

Rotation on the Coordinate Plane

When performing a rotation on the coordinate plane, the center of rotation remains fixed, and all points of the figure rotate a specified number of degrees around that point.

For example, rotating a square around the origin by 90 degrees would result in a new orientation, but the size of the square stays the same.

Important Notes

Rotation transformations are widely used in geometry, art, and design. Understanding how to rotate figures is essential for visualizing patterns and creating symmetrical structures.
