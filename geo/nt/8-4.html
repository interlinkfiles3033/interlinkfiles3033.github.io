8.4 Triangles - Definition, Classification, Interactives, and Examples

Big Picture

A **triangle** is a polygon with three sides and three angles. Triangles are classified in two main ways: by their **sides** (Equilateral, Isosceles, and Scalene) and by their **angles** (Acute, Right, and Obtuse). Understanding these classifications helps to apply triangle properties in real-world geometry problems such as architecture, design, and navigation.

Key Terms

  • Triangle: A polygon with three sides and three angles.
  • Parallel Lines: Two or more lines that lie in the same plane and never intersect. These lines will always have the same slope.
  • Angles: A geometric figure formed by two rays that connect at a single point or vertex.
  • Corresponding: The corresponding sides between two triangles are sides in the same relative position.
  • Right Angle: An angle equal to 90 degrees.
  • Equiangular Triangle: A triangle with all congruent angles, each measuring 60 degrees.
  • Equilateral Triangle: A triangle where all three sides are equal in length and it is also equiangular.
  • Congruent: Identical in size, shape, and measure. Congruence can occur even if a shape is rotated or flipped.
  • Right Triangle: A triangle with exactly one right angle (90 degrees). The sides adjacent to the right angle are called legs, and the side opposite the right angle is the hypotenuse.
  • Exterior Angle: An angle formed by extending one side of the triangle.
  • Isosceles Triangle: A triangle in which exactly two sides are of equal length.

Important Info

- The **interior angles** of any triangle always sum to 180°. - In a **right triangle**, the Pythagorean theorem holds: 

a² + b² = c²
where **a** and **b** are the legs, and **c** is the hypotenuse.

- **Equilateral triangles** have all sides equal, and all angles measure 60°. - **Isosceles triangles** have two sides of equal length, and two equal angles. - **Scalene triangles** have all sides and all angles of different measures.

Example

Let's calculate the third side of a **right triangle** using the Pythagorean theorem. If one leg measures **3 cm** and the other leg is **4 cm**, we can find the length of the hypotenuse **c**: 

a² + b² = c²
3² + 4² = c²
9 + 16 = c²
25 = c²
c = √25
c = 5 cm
Therefore, the hypotenuse of the triangle is **5 cm**.

Interactive Example

To better understand the classification of triangles:

  • Draw an **Equilateral Triangle** where all sides are the same length and measure all the angles to verify they are each 60°.
  • Draw an **Isosceles Triangle** and identify the two equal sides and angles.
  • Use a **protractor** to measure the angles of a **right triangle** and confirm that one angle is exactly 90°.

**Tip**: Using **triangle inequalities** is important: for any triangle, the sum of the lengths of any two sides must always be greater than the length of the third side.

Conclusion

Triangles are fundamental in geometry and are classified based on their **angles** and **sides**. Understanding the properties of different types of triangles, such as **equilateral**, **isosceles**, and **right triangles**, helps us solve geometric problems and apply them in various fields, including architecture and engineering. The Pythagorean theorem and triangle inequality are important tools for working with triangles.
