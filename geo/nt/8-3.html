8.3 Polygons - Definition, Types, Properties, Interactives and Examples

Big Picture

A **polygon** is a shape bounded by a number of straight lines. Polygons are usually classified by the number of sides they have and their properties. The simplest polygon is a **triangle**, with three sides, while other polygons can have more sides, such as **quadrilaterals**, **pentagons**, and so on.

Understanding polygons involves knowing their number of sides, angles, and properties, which are important in geometry and real-world applications like architecture, engineering, and design.

Key Terms

  • Polygon: A simple closed figure with at least three straight sides.
  • Triangle: A polygon with three sides and three angles.
  • Quadrilateral: A polygon with four sides and four vertices.
  • Pentagon: A polygon with five sides.
  • Hexagon: A polygon with six sides.
  • Heptagon: A polygon with seven sides.
  • Octagon: A polygon with eight sides.
  • Nonagon (Enneagon): A polygon with nine sides.
  • Decagon: A polygon with ten sides.
  • Diagonal: A line segment in a polygon that connects non-consecutive vertices.
  • Line Segment: A finite part of a line with two endpoints.
  • Vertices: The points where the sides of a polygon meet.
  • Convex Polygon: A polygon where no interior angle is greater than 180 degrees.
  • Concave Polygon: A polygon with at least one interior angle greater than 180 degrees.
  • Interior Angles: The angles inside a polygon.
  • Exterior Angles: Angles formed by extending a side of a polygon.
  • Straight Angle: An angle that measures 180 degrees.
  • Circle: The set of all points at a fixed distance (radius) from a center point.

Important Info

- A **polygon** is any closed figure made up of straight line segments, where the line segments are called sides. The points where the sides meet are called **vertices**.

- The sum of the interior angles of a polygon can be calculated with the formula: 

Sum of interior angles = (n - 2) × 180°
where **n** is the number of sides.

- For **convex polygons**, all interior angles are less than 180°. - **Concave polygons** have at least one angle greater than 180°, often creating a "caved-in" appearance.

- The sum of the exterior angles of any polygon is always 360°.

Example

Let's consider a **hexagon** (six-sided polygon). To find the sum of its interior angles, we use the formula for the sum of interior angles: 

Sum of interior angles = (n - 2) × 180°
Sum of interior angles = (6 - 2) × 180°
Sum of interior angles = 4 × 180°
Sum of interior angles = 720°
Therefore, the sum of the interior angles of a hexagon is **720°**.

Interactive Example

To visualize and understand more about polygons:

  • Draw polygons on a grid, starting with triangles, quadrilaterals, and pentagons.
  • Measure the interior angles using a **protractor** and verify the sum using the formula.
  • Explore **concave** and **convex** polygons by drawing them and noting the difference in angles.

**Tip**: You can use **diagonal lines** in the polygons to break them into triangles and calculate their area as a sum of triangle areas.

Conclusion

Understanding **polygons** is essential in geometry as they form the basis of many geometric shapes and designs. By studying their **types**, **properties**, and **angles**, we can solve geometric problems and apply these concepts in real-world scenarios like architecture, design, and art. Using formulas for **interior** and **exterior angles** helps in determining important characteristics of polygons in both 2D and 3D spaces.
